The Brief

A self-initiated LinkedIn sports marketing campaign series — built from zero to 700+ impressions with no budget, no team, no brief.

700+Launch Week Impressions

281Unique Members Reached
The Problem

How do you prove strategic thinking without a client brief?

Most early-career marketers can talk about strategy. Few can demonstrate it publicly, against real metrics, without a client or agency backing them. I needed to close that gap — not with a course certificate, but with actual campaign output.

The Strategy

Build a content brand, not just content.

The Brief was designed as a weekly campaign series — not a blog, not random posts. Each edition follows the same structure: defined subject, strategic angle, data anchor, clear POV, and a branded visual card. The consistency was the strategy. A repeatable format builds audience trust the same way a campaign builds brand recall.

The Execution

Brief → produce → publish → analyze → optimize.

Every edition ran the same agency workflow I use for paid clients. Week 1: Toronto FC — leaned into the FIFA 2026 World Cup angle at BMO Field and Toronto's multicultural identity. The launch post hit 700+ impressions. Week 4 shifted to data-heavy framing after noticing data-led angles outperformed opinion content. The PWHL post (292 impressions in 3 days) became the proof of concept for this hypothesis.

The Results

700+ impressions. +186% week-over-week. Zero paid budget.

471 impressions in the last 7 days with +186% WoW growth. 281 unique members reached. Best post: PWHL analysis, 292 impressions in 3 days — proof that data-driven editorial outperforms opinion content with a sports marketing audience.

The Lesson

Data-led angles beat opinion every time.

The clearest learning: posts anchored in data and commercial analysis consistently outperformed opinion-led takes. The audience responds to numbers with a clear strategic interpretation — not hot takes. That's the same insight that drives effective content strategy for any brand.

The Insight

The fit isn't forced — it's obvious.

Toronto FC's fanbase speaks dozens of languages. The World Cup brings 32 nations to BMO Field. Duolingo's brand is built on making language accessible to everyone. Three brands aligned on the same cultural moment — none of them had activated the overlap.

The Concept

Four activation pillars.

"Word of the Match" — a weekly co-branded Reel where a TFC player teaches one football term in their native language. Duolingo owl sleeve patch — visible every match, every broadcast, every highlight clip. June 12 Activation — a free Duolingo course drops to every Canadian user on Canada's World Cup home game day. TFC Kickstart Community Program — youth clinics with Duolingo language kits across Toronto.

The Deliverables

Full campaign output, published live.

Sponsorship activation concept, 4-week social calendar, campaign brief, partnership pitch deck, and post-campaign recap — all built and published independently at mkore21.github.io/tfc-portfolio.

Brand Strategy · 2026

The Cold
City.

Spotify × Toronto Raptors × Drake Iceman — a campaign connecting three converging forces that no brand activated.

The Gap

Same identity. Different reality.

Drake's Iceman album dropped May 2026. He attended the Raptors' final game with ice-covered courtside seats. The Raptors are the Iceman's team in aesthetic — cold, deliberate, calculated. But the product on the floor doesn't match the brand being projected. Drake: Frozen by design. Raptors: Frozen by default. The gap between the two is a campaign.

The Strategy

Co-branded playlists, player content, in-arena activation, OVO merch.

Spotify co-branded "Ice Night" playlist tied to Iceman drops. Player content series using Drake's album aesthetic. In-arena activation with Iceman visual language. OVO × Raptors limited merch drop. The campaign doesn't ignore the rebuild — it uses the Iceman philosophy to set the standard the franchise needs to meet.

The Lesson

The best brand gaps are the ones brands can't see themselves.

The Raptors, Drake, and Spotify are too close to the situation to see the narrative gap clearly. An external eye — the kind of perspective a strategist brings — spots the opportunity that's invisible from inside the brand.
